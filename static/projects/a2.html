Part 2: Analysis

To explore the role of Corporate Landlordship in Boston, an examination of data on corporate landlord rates from both the second and third datasets was undertaken. It became evident that corporate landlordship has experienced a significant surge since 2004 across various neighborhoods, while Owner Occupancy rates have either stagnated or possibly declined.

This surge in corporate landlordship indicates a recent phenomenon. To assess its effects, I devised the "Corporate Surplus" metric, calculated as Corporate Ownership Rate minus Owner Occupancy Rate, to gauge the state of corporate rentals in each neighborhood.

Corporate Surplus = Corporate Own Rate - Owner Occ Rate

Corporate Surplus serves as a barometer of the housing market, contrasting Corporate Owned Rentals with Owner Occupied properties, which represent opposing ends of the spectrum. This metric underscores the dynamic interplay between these two housing sectors.

In essence, three distinct types of housing can be delineated:

  1. Corporate Owned Rentals: These units are owned by corporations, as denoted by the Corp Own Rate in the data.
  2. Private Owned Rentals: These units, presumably owned by individuals, constitute the remaining segment of housing unaccounted for in the data.
  3. Owner Occupied: These units are occupied by the property owner, as indicated by the Own Occ Rate in the data.

To assess the impact of corporate landlordship on neighborhoods, an analysis of the change in Corporate Surplus since 2004 was deemed crucial.

Above is a bar chart depicting Boston neighborhoods ranked by their change in Corporate Surplus since 2004.

This measure signifies the shift in Corporate Landlordship's market share relative to individual property ownership. For instance, the West End's 39% indicates that since 2004, Corporate Landlordship has gained a 39% market share compared to owning one's property.

Upon scrutinizing the most affected neighborhoods, several discernible patterns emerge.

The top 5 neighborhoods predominantly comprise urban areas. This alignment with urban locales mirrors the transition towards corporate landlordship, typically associated with apartment complexes over single-family homes.

Additionally, these urban neighborhoods tend to be inhabited by young professionals and college students, who often reside in large apartment complexes proximal to their workplaces or universities, which are more frequently corporate-owned.

Further, when comparing the corporate landlordship rates to the map of Boston eviction filings, we get a different conclusion

The first map, sourced from an article by HomesForAllMass, illustrates the racial demographics of Boston neighborhoods juxtaposed with their eviction filings.

The second map, crafted by me, displays Corporate Landlordship rates across Boston.

If corporate landlords were solely responsible for evictions, we would anticipate an alignment between the yellow dots on the first map and the shaded areas on the second. This observation doesn't imply that corporate landlords are inherently more lenient or preferable. Rather, it underscores the correlation between Corporate Landlordship and urbanization. Residents in urban areas, typically served by corporate-owned multi-unit buildings, tend to face fewer eviction threats due to factors such as short-term leasing practices (common among young professionals) or robust rent protections (common in university-dominated areas)
